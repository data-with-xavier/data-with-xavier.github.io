Chart Selection Essentials

One question before you open your charting tool

Before you pick a chart type, ask: what relationship am I trying to show? Almost every chart falls into one of four categories based on the relationship it reveals.

The four categories:
— Comparison: How do values rank or change against each other?
— Relationship: Is there a connection between two or more variables?
— Composition: What makes up the whole?
— Distribution: How are values spread across a range?

Match your question to the right category, then pick the chart within that category that fits your data. The sections below walk through every major chart type.

Category 1

Comparison

Use a comparison chart when you want to show how values rank against each other — at a point in time, or how they change over time.

Click any chart type to see a live example.

A bar chart uses horizontal bars to compare values across categories. Bar length is proportional to value, and horizontal layout gives long category labels room to breathe.

Use when: Ranking discrete categories — especially when labels are long. Sorting longest-to-shortest hands the reader the ranking immediately.
Avoid when: Showing change over time (use a line chart) or parts of a whole (use stacked bar or pie).

Q4 Revenue by Product Line ($K) — Sorted so the ranking is immediate. Eyes travel left-to-right along each bar with no math required.

A column chart is the vertical sibling of the bar chart. It's the default for comparing values across a few categories — especially when the x-axis is time, since left-to-right matches how we read chronology.

Use when: Comparing ≤8 categories with short labels, or when the x-axis is time-ordered (months, quarters, years).
Avoid when: You have many categories or long labels — switch to a bar chart. Also avoid connecting bars with a line if the x-axis isn't continuous.

Monthly Sales ($K), Jan–Jun — Time on the x-axis, values on the y-axis. The upward trend is visible in the column heights.

A line chart connects data points to show trend over a continuous axis — usually time. The slope of the line communicates rate of change instantly. Multiple lines compare trends across groups in the same space.

Use when: Showing how a value changes over a continuous sequence. The shape of the line IS the insight — momentum, acceleration, dips, and plateaus all read instantly.
Avoid when: The x-axis is categorical with no natural order (use a bar chart), or you have only 2 data points (use a slope chart — it's cleaner).

Monthly Active Users, Jan–Dec — The line shows not just growth but its shape: steady climb with a slight dip in September worth investigating.

A slope chart is a line chart reduced to exactly two time points. Each line connects a "before" value to an "after" value. Rising slope = increase. Falling slope = decrease. The steepness shows how dramatic the change was.

Use when: Comparing change between exactly two periods across multiple entities — market share 2023 vs 2024 for five competitors, before/after a product change.
Avoid when: You have more than two time points (use a line chart) or so many entities that lines cross repeatedly into an unreadable tangle.

Market Share (%): 2023 vs 2024 — Rising lines gained share, falling lines lost it. No math needed — the direction and steepness tell the whole story.

A bullet chart compares a primary measure (actual) against a target and qualitative performance bands (poor / satisfactory / good). Invented by Stephen Few as a compact, information-dense alternative to gauges and speedometers. Multiple bullet charts stack compactly for KPI dashboards.

Use when: Showing performance vs. a target — sales quotas, project milestones, KPI dashboards. Packs actual + target + performance context into minimal space.
Avoid when: Your audience has never seen one — explain it briefly the first time. For simple target vs. actual with a general audience, a grouped bar chart may communicate faster.

Q4 Sales vs $100K Target by Rep — Purple = actual, gray = target. Ada and Eli are above quota; Ben is the one to watch.

A radar chart (spider or web chart) plots multiple variables on axes radiating from a center point. Each entity forms a polygon. The shape of the polygon is the fingerprint of that entity's strengths and weaknesses across all dimensions simultaneously.

Use when: Comparing 2–3 entities across 4–8 dimensions — product comparisons, performance reviews, team skill assessments. The overall shape communicates the profile at a glance.
Avoid when: You have only one entity (no comparison = no radar), more than 3 entities (polygons overlap into a mess), or when precise values matter (use a bar chart — it's more accurate).

Product A vs Product B — 6 Dimensions — Product A's shape is wider on Speed and Price; Product B's extends further on Support and Design. The profiles tell a story bars couldn't.

Category 2

Relationship

Use a relationship chart when you want to explore or show connections between variables — correlation, flow, or co-variation across multiple dimensions.

Click any chart type to see a live example.

A scatter plot places each data point at the intersection of two numeric variables. The pattern of dots reveals correlation (move together), inverse correlation (one up, one down), or no relationship at all. Outliers stand out visually with no effort.

Use when: Exploring whether two continuous variables are related. Great for finding clusters, outliers, and correlation across medium-to-large datasets (15+ points).
Avoid when: You have fewer than ~15 points (no pattern will emerge) or when your goal is to communicate a single summary statistic — show the number directly.

Ad Spend ($K) vs Conversions — The upward drift suggests a positive correlation. The outlier at top-left (high conversions, low spend) is worth investigating.

A bubble chart extends the scatter plot by encoding a third variable as the size of each dot. Three dimensions in one view: x position, y position, and bubble area. Color can add a fourth. Common in strategy charts like the BCG matrix.

Use when: You need to show three variables at once and the third variable has meaningful size differences. Works well with ≤15 bubbles before the chart becomes cluttered.
Avoid when: Bubble sizes are similar (the encoding becomes meaningless) or precision matters (humans are poor at judging area differences — use a bar chart for the third variable instead).

Ad Spend vs Conversions, sized by Campaign Budget — Same x/y as the scatter plot, but now bubble size shows how much budget was behind each campaign.

A heatmap uses color intensity to encode a value across a matrix of two categorical axes. The result is a grid where each cell's color communicates the magnitude at that intersection. Strong patterns emerge as blocks of similar color.

Use when: Showing how a metric varies across two categorical dimensions simultaneously — sessions by day × hour, correlation matrices, feature usage by user segment.
Avoid when: Precise values matter (color is an imprecise encoding), or one axis has only 2–3 categories (a grouped bar chart will be clearer).

Website Sessions by Day × Hour — Darker purple = more sessions. Weekday midday is the clear peak; weekends are quiet. The pattern is readable in under a second.

A Sankey chart shows flow of quantities between nodes. The width of each band is proportional to the quantity flowing. Originally used for energy systems; now common for marketing funnels, budget allocations, and user journey analysis.

Use when: Showing how a quantity is distributed or transferred across stages — conversion funnels, budget flows, user path analysis. The shrinking bands make drop-off visible instantly.
Avoid when: You have too many nodes or too many crossing flows — it becomes unreadable. Keep to ≤8 nodes and ≤12 flows.

Marketing Funnel: 1,000 Prospects — The shrinking band widths show where people drop off at each stage. The biggest loss is Awareness → Interest.

Category 3

Composition

Use a composition chart when your question is about parts of a whole — what percentage does each piece contribute, and how do those proportions change?

Click any chart type to see a live example.

A pie chart divides a circle into slices proportional to each category's share. A donut chart removes the center — which reduces area-encoding distortion and creates space for a summary statistic. Both work only when all parts genuinely sum to 100%.

Use when: Showing simple part-to-whole composition with 2–4 categories. Most powerful when one slice is dramatically dominant — the visual contrast IS the story.
Avoid when: You have more than 4–5 categories (humans can't judge slice angles accurately), slices are similar in size, or you need to compare across multiple wholes (use a stacked bar instead).

Revenue by Region (% of total) — Four slices, clear dominant. North's share is obvious without calculating. A stacked bar would be needed to compare this across years.

A stacked bar chart stacks multiple series to show both the total and each part's contribution. A 100% stacked bar normalizes all bars to the same height and shows proportions only — useful when the totals differ but the share is what matters.

Use when: Showing how composition changes across categories or over time. Use regular stacked for total + mix; use 100% stacked when only the proportion matters.
Avoid when: You have more than 4–5 series — middle segments don't share a common baseline, making them impossible to compare. Highlight one segment with a separate chart instead.

Revenue by Product Category ($K), Q1–Q4 — Bar height shows total revenue; colors show how the product mix shifts quarter by quarter.

A treemap encodes composition as nested rectangles. Each rectangle's area is proportional to its value. It's highly space-efficient — you can show dozens of categories where a bar chart would need dozens of rows.

Use when: Showing the composition of a large hierarchy where relative size is the story — budget breakdowns, portfolio views, product revenue with many line items.
Avoid when: Precise comparison matters (area is an imprecise encoding) or all values are similar (the rectangles become nearly identical and lose meaning).

Annual Budget by Department — Area = budget. Engineering and Sales dominate immediately. HR and Legal are visible but clearly smaller — no sorting or scrolling needed.

A waterfall chart shows how a starting value builds up or breaks down through a series of positive and negative contributions to reach a final value. Increases float up from the previous bar; decreases float down. The first and last bars are totals.

Use when: Explaining the journey from one value to another through its components — profit bridges, budget variances, headcount changes. The narrative is "here's how we got there."
Avoid when: The components don't sum to a meaningful total, or when you have more than ~8 steps (the chart becomes hard to follow). A table may be clearer for very granular breakdowns.

Profit Bridge: $500K Revenue → $180K Net Profit — Each bar floats from the previous level. Green = gains, red = costs. The journey from revenue to profit is told in one view.

Category 4

Distribution

Use a distribution chart when your question is about the spread of values — where do most values cluster, how wide is the range, and are there outliers?

Click any chart type to see a live example.

A histogram groups continuous values into bins and counts how many fall in each bin. The shape of the bars reveals the distribution — symmetric, skewed, bimodal, or with outliers. Unlike bar charts, the bars touch because the x-axis is continuous, with no gaps between bins.

Use when: Understanding the distribution of a single continuous variable — customer ages, order values, response times, test scores. The shape of the histogram is the insight.
Avoid when: You have fewer than ~30 values (no meaningful shape will emerge), or your variable is categorical (use a bar chart). Bin size has a huge impact — always try multiple sizes.

Distribution of Customer Order Values ($) — Most orders cluster $20–$60. The right skew reveals a long tail of high-value orders that deserve separate analysis.

A box plot (box-and-whisker) summarizes a distribution using five statistics: minimum, Q1 (25th percentile), median, Q3 (75th percentile), and maximum. The box is the middle 50% of values. Whiskers extend to min/max. Individual outliers appear as dots beyond the whiskers.

Use when: Comparing distributions across multiple groups side by side — test scores by class, salaries by department, delivery times by carrier. A box plot packs a full distribution summary into very little space.
Avoid when: Your audience doesn't know what quartiles are — box plots require a brief explanation for general audiences. A histogram or bar chart of medians may communicate faster.

Test Score Distribution by Class — Class B has the highest median. Class A is tighter (more consistent results). Outlier dots show individual unusual scores worth following up.
