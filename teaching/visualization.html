What is a data visualization actually for?

A spreadsheet stores numbers. A visualization makes those numbers mean something. Our eyes are astonishingly good at spotting patterns — a rising line, a tall bar, an outlier dot — far faster than they can read a column of figures. A good chart borrows that power to answer a question or move an argument forward.

But that same power cuts both ways. The very thing that makes a chart persuasive — its instant, gut-level impact — is also what makes a bad chart dangerous. A misleading visual gets believed before anyone checks the math. So as we go, hold two questions in your head:

The two questions for every chart: (1) Is it clear? — can someone understand it in a few seconds without a tour guide? (2) Is it honest? — does the visual impression match what the data actually says? Almost everything below is in service of these two questions.

Framework 1

Edward Tufte's Principles

Edward Tufte is the godfather of modern data visualization. His core belief: a graphic exists to show the data — everything else is decoration that gets in the way. His nine principles all push toward charts that are dense with information and honest about what they show.

The data — not borders, backgrounds, or styling — is the point. Tufte's data-ink ratio asks: of all the "ink" on a chart, how much actually represents numbers? Push it as high as you can. Same data below (monthly website visitors), two amounts of decoration.

✗ Cluttered

Why it fails: Heavy gridlines, a glowing fill, fat markers, and a shouting title all compete with the one thing that matters — the trend. Your eye has to dig through the decoration to find the data.

✓ Clean

Why it works: Strip away everything that isn't data. One clean line, faint guides, a clear label. The steady 62% climb is now the only thing you notice.

A good chart makes you think about the subject, not the design — and the best ones surface something that makes you ask "why?" Here's the same factory's defect data shown two ways.

✗ Dead end

Why it fails: A single summary total — "1,240 defects" — provokes no thought. There's nothing to wonder about, nothing to investigate, no question raised.

✓ Raises a question

Why it works: Showing defects by month surfaces a sharp March spike. Instantly you ask "what happened in March?" — that's thought provoked, and it points to where the real work is.

The visual size of an effect must match the size of the effect in the numbers. The classic offender is the truncated y-axis. These are identical satisfaction scores (all near 89/100) — only the axis start changes.

✗ Distorted

Why it fails: The axis starts at 88, so a change under one point looks like a towering win — Q4 appears ~5× taller than Q1. Tufte calls this a high "lie factor."

✓ Truthful

Why it works: Starting at 0 makes the bars nearly identical — which is the truth. Less dramatic, and correct: the drama wasn't real.

Watch for this everywhere. Truncated axes are constant in ads, earnings slides, and the news. Always check where a bar chart's axis starts — and if it isn't zero, ask why.

A chart can be information-rich. The same box can show four numbers or sixty — earn the reader's attention with density rather than wasting space.

✗ Sparse

Why it fails: Four quarterly totals fill an entire chart. That's barely worth the ink — a single sentence would do the same job.

✓ Dense

Why it works: Five product lines across twelve months — 60 data points in the same space — and the patterns are still readable. Density rewards a closer look.

When you have lots of data, help the reader see structure they could never get reading row by row. Below: store size vs. sales for 20 stores.

✗ Noise

Why it fails: Twenty identical gray dots — a shapeless cloud. There's data here, but no story you can grab onto.

✓ Structure

Why it works: Coloring the same points by store type reveals the pattern instantly — rural stores cluster low and never catch up. The cloud becomes a finding.

Arrange the data so comparisons are easy and natural. Try to rank these six regions from largest to smallest in each chart.

✗ Hard to compare

Why it fails: Humans are terrible at judging angles and wedge areas. With six similar slices, which is biggest? You can't tell without reading every label.

✓ Easy to compare

Why it works: Length on a common baseline is the easiest comparison there is. Sorting longest-to-shortest hands the reader the ranking for free.

Great charts work at the overview level and reward a closer look with detail. This layering is the backbone of every good dashboard.

✗ One level only

Why it fails: "$1.24M for the year" gives you the headline and nothing else. There's no way to drill in or see how you got there.

✓ Overview + detail

Why it works: The same annual total, broken into months. You see the overall shape at a glance and can still read any single month.

Every chart should answer a question. If you can't name the question it answers, cut the chart.

✗ No question

Why it fails: Revenue, headcount, and office temperature on one axis. Three unrelated metrics, no shared scale, no question — just because the data existed.

✓ Clear question

Why it works: It answers exactly one question — did revenue grow every quarter? — and the answer (yes) is obvious in a second.

Charts don't work alone. A title, a labeled axis, and a stated statistic turn a picture into an argument the reader can follow and check.

✗ Picture only

Why it fails: A rising shape with no words. Of what? Measured how? The reader has to guess the point — and may guess wrong.

✓ Picture + words

Why it works: A declarative title states the finding, the subtitle gives the key statistic and source, and the axes are labeled. The chart and the words reinforce each other.

Framework 2

Stephen Few's Principles

Stephen Few took Tufte's ideas and sharpened them for the world of business dashboards. His rules are practical and specific — mostly about not letting the visual say something the numbers don't.

Click any principle to open a live good-vs-bad example. Hover the charts for details.

Include exactly what serves the message. Too much buries the point; too little hides it. The story here is "organic search is taking off" — but the data has eight traffic channels.

✗ Too much

Why it fails: Eight lines and an eight-item legend. The one line that matters is lost in a tangle of seven that don't.

✓ Just enough

Why it works: Keep the other channels as faint context, but spend the bright color on Organic. The reader sees the point and still has the context.

If two marks look different, the data behind them should differ by the same amount. Encoding a value as a circle's radius quietly breaks this. The three values are 10, 20, and 30.

✗ Area lies

Why it fails: Setting radius equal to the value means the value of 30 covers nine times the area of the value of 10 — because area grows with the square of the radius. The eye reads area, so it reads a 9× gap that isn't there.

✓ Length is honest

Why it works: A bar 3× as long means 3× the value. Length maps to value one-to-one — no hidden exaggeration.

Humans read length and position far more accurately than color shade. Encode the number with the channel people read best.

✗ Color only

Why it fails: Every bar is the same height; the value is buried in a shade of purple. Quick — who's the top rep? You'd have to squint and guess. (Hover to see the real numbers.)

✓ Length encodes

Why it works: The value is now the bar height. Cody's lead is unmistakable, and you can rank all five instantly.

The size of the visual difference should match the size of the real difference. These four competitors are all within 2% of each other (~$31M).

✗ Exaggerated

Why it fails: With the axis starting at $30.4M, a 2% lead looks like a landslide. The visual difference dwarfs the real difference.

✓ Proportional

Why it works: From a zero baseline the bars are nearly equal — because the companies are nearly equal. The picture matches the math.

A line implies a relationship between points. Never draw one across unrelated categories — Coffee doesn't "lead to" Tea.

✗ False trend

Why it fails: The line implies sales "fall" from Coffee to Tea and "recover" at Pastries. But the category order is arbitrary — those slopes are pure noise.

✓ Separate categories

Why it works: Bars treat each category as its own thing. Rule: lines are for things that flow (time); bars are for things that don't (categories).

Guide the eye. Make the one thing that matters louder than everything else. Here the point is the new December record.

✗ Flat emphasis

Why it fails: Every point looks equally important, so nothing stands out. The reader has to hunt for the takeaway.

✓ Directed

Why it works: Muting the line and lighting up only the final point pulls the eye straight to "December hit a record." The emphasis does the talking.

Framework 3

Conzet's Principles (yes, I added my own)

After years of building dashboards, I've boiled my own working philosophy down to six rules. They lean on Tufte and Few but emphasize the part that's easy to forget: a chart is an act of communication, and communication always has an audience and a point.

Click any principle to open a live good-vs-bad example. Hover the charts for details.

A chart should move your story along. A static snapshot rarely does; a comparison that makes a point does.

✗ Snapshot

Why it fails: "We converted 32% this month" is true — but it goes nowhere. There's no comparison, no direction, no story.

✓ Makes a point

Why it works: Before vs. after the redesign carries a narrative: the change worked — conversion jumped 50%. The chart argues something.

If your chart is harder to read than the table it replaced, you've gone backwards. Below: a cafe rated on six attributes.

✗ Harder than a table

Why it fails: A radar chart makes you decode angles, directions, and overlapping shapes just to compare six numbers. It looks sophisticated and reads terribly.

✓ Instantly clear

Why it works: The same six numbers as bars. Highest to lowest in one glance — no decoding required.

Don't decorate. Don't show off. Every design choice should point the eye at the insight. The story: Team C is the standout.

✗ Decoration

Why it fails: Five colors shout "five equally important things!" The color means nothing and spreads your attention evenly — the opposite of focus.

✓ Focus

Why it works: Mute everything, then spend one bright color on what matters. The eye jumps to Team C. Color became information: "look here."

Accessibility note: ~8% of men have red-green color blindness. Don't rely on red-vs-green alone for "good vs. bad" — pair color with labels or position so the message survives without it.

Omitting a title, a unit, a time frame, or a source can mislead as badly as faking the data. Same signups line — one version refuses to tell you what it is.

✗ No context

Why it fails: A line going up — of what? Over what period? Versus what goal? With no title, units, or source, the reader can invent any story. (Hover it — even the tooltip won't help.)

✓ Full context

Why it works: A declarative title states the takeaway, the axis carries units, the time frame is explicit, and a source builds trust. Now the reader can check the claim.

Titles that make an argument. "Monthly Signups" just labels the chart. "New Signups Nearly Tripled in Six Months" tells the reader what to think — and uses the chart as proof. Write the headline, not the label.

Simple is a choice, not laziness. Strip a complex chart down to the single thing your audience actually needs. The message: support tickets are falling.

✗ Overbuilt

Why it fails: Seven stacked categories. Nobody can track any single band, and the "are tickets going up or down?" question gets lost in the layers.

✓ Simplified

Why it works: If the message is "tickets are falling," one total line says it cleanly. The breakdown can live in a table for whoever needs it.

Design for whoever is actually reading. An analyst wants the detail; an executive wants the answer. Same underlying performance, two audiences.

✗ Wrong audience

Why it fails: A jargon-titled scatter of weekly variance by cohort is great for an analyst — and useless in a 30-second executive readout. Right data, wrong room.

✓ Right audience

Why it works: "We hit 112% of target" in two bars is exactly what an executive needs — the answer, instantly. Keep the scatter for the analyst who'll dig in.

Putting It Together

Choosing the right visualization

Most chart mistakes start before any pixels are drawn — they start with picking the wrong chart type. The trick is to start from the question, not the chart. What relationship in the data are you trying to show? Match it to the chart built for that job.

If you want to show…Use…Beginner tip
Comparison across categoriesBar chartUse horizontal bars when labels are long. Sort by value unless the order is meaningful.
Change over timeLine chartTime on the x-axis, left to right. Never use a line for non-time categories.
Relationship between two numbersScatter plotGreat for spotting correlation — e.g., ad spend vs. sales.
Parts of a wholeBar (or sparingly, pie)Only reach for a pie with 2–4 slices and one obvious point. Otherwise, bars.
Composition and totals over timeStacked barGood for "how does the mix change month to month?"
Many categories, one pattern eachSmall multiplesA grid of tiny identical charts beats one chart with six overlapping lines.
The pie chart trap: When in doubt, don't use a pie. A bar chart almost always communicates the same "part of a whole" idea more clearly, because length is easier to judge than angle.
